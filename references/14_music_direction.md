# Music Direction

Music is a production requirement, not an afterthought. Every script must include music direction specific enough for a producer to search a licensing platform and come back with the right options.

"Inspiring music" is not direction. It is a wish.

## What good music direction looks like

Bad: "Upbeat music plays."
Good: "Driving electronic, 120 BPM, layered synths with crisp percussion. Builds through the first 30 seconds, drops to bass and rhythm at the product reveal, then rebuilds to a full mix for the close."

Bad: "Emotional underscore."
Good: "Solo piano, 70 BPM, sparse and deliberate. Strings enter at :30 as the story turns. No crescendo until the final 10 seconds. Leave room for VO to carry the weight."

Bad: "Fun, energetic track."
Good: "Funk-influenced pop, 110 BPM, groove-driven bass line with claps on the 2 and 4. Light brass stabs. Playful, not frantic. Think commercial kitchen energy."

## Search language vocabulary

These are terms music licensing platforms actually understand. Use them.

### Tempo
- Slow: 60 to 80 BPM
- Mid-tempo: 80 to 110 BPM
- Upbeat: 110 to 130 BPM
- Fast: 130+ BPM

### Energy descriptors
- Sparse, minimal, breathing, restrained
- Building, accumulating, layering, rising
- Driving, propulsive, relentless, locked-in
- Explosive, peak, full mix, all-in
- Resolving, landing, settling, exhale

### Instrument vocabulary
- Percussion-forward (drums lead the feel)
- Synth-driven (electronic textures carry the track)
- Acoustic-led (guitar, piano, strings in natural recording)
- Orchestral (strings, brass, woodwinds, composed feel)
- Hybrid (electronic + acoustic blend)
- Sound design-driven (textures, risers, impacts, no traditional instruments)

### Genre terms that work for search
- Ambient electronic
- Cinematic orchestral
- Indie folk / acoustic
- Modern classical / neo-classical
- Hip-hop influenced (trap percussion, 808s)
- Lo-fi / downtempo
- Pop / commercial pop
- Funk / soul-influenced
- Tropical / island-influenced
- Tension / suspense
- Corporate modern (light and clean, minimal personality)

### Mood and texture terms
- Warm, golden, sunlit
- Cold, clinical, precise
- Gritty, raw, textured
- Polished, clean, bright
- Dark, brooding, heavy
- Hopeful, open, ascending
- Nostalgic, analog, vintage

## Energy curves by tone

Map music energy to the script's emotional arc. Music should mirror the structure, not just sit underneath it.

### Bold / High-Energy
Start at medium-high. Build aggressively. Hit a peak at the key reveal or data moment. Brief drop for contrast. Return to full energy for the close. The music never fully relaxes.

### Confident / Corporate Clean
Start clean and steady. Subtle build through each section. No dramatic drops or peaks. The music supports without competing for attention. Slight lift at the close, not a crescendo.

### Warm / Human
Start sparse. Acoustic elements enter naturally. Build warmth through layering, not volume. The music should feel like it showed up, not like it was produced. Peak is gentle. Close is soft.

### Reflective / Cinematic
Start with space. Long tones, sustained notes, room between elements. Build slowly over the full runtime. The crescendo, if there is one, arrives late and earns its moment. Never rush.

### Playful / Light
Start with groove. The beat is immediate and inviting. Music is forward in the mix. Energy stays consistent with small variations. Close is clean and tight, not a fadeout.

### Urgent / Rally
Start tense. Sparse percussion, low tones, pressure. Layer elements as proof mounts. The turn from problem to solution should coincide with a musical shift from tension to resolve. Close with full, earned release.

## Music transition logic

Music must bridge sections, not just accompany them.

- **Between pillars or chapters:** Use a filter sweep, a drum fill, or a brief drop to mark the shift. The music should signal "new section" without fully resetting.
- **At the problem-to-solution turn:** This is where the music changes character, not just volume. A tonal shift (minor to major, sparse to full, tension to release) tells the audience the story has turned.
- **At the close:** The last 5 to 10 seconds of music must feel like an ending, not a fade. Resolve the musical idea. Land it.

## Music types for production

### Underscore
Music sits behind VO and visuals. Never competes. Works for explainers, case studies, corporate films. The music is felt, not noticed.

### Featured track
Music is a primary element. VO works around it or is minimal. Works for sizzle reels, event openers, brand anthems. The track has personality and presence.

### Sound design-driven
No traditional music. Textures, risers, impacts, environmental sound, designed audio. Works for tech reveals, AI pieces, tension-heavy films. Every sound is intentional.

### Hybrid
Combination of composed music and sound design elements. Useful for pieces that need both emotional warmth and modern edge. Common in brand platform launches.

## Common mistakes

- Writing "music swells" at the close of every script. Not every piece earns a swell. Some should end quiet.
- Ignoring music in the middle sections. Music direction is needed throughout, not just at the open and close.
- Specifying a specific commercial song ("Something like 'Feeling Good' by Nina Simone"). This creates licensing expectations. Describe the feel instead.
- Letting music and VO compete. If the VO is dense, the music must be sparse. If the music is featured, the VO must step back. They cannot both be loud.
