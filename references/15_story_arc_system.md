# 15. Story Arc System

## Why this file exists

A brief gives you a topic. A topic is not a story.

"Coca-Cola Zero Sugar has a new formula" is a topic. It is information. It sits flat on the page. It does not move.

"People dismissed Zero Sugar for years. Then the formula changed. Now it is the fastest-growing cola in the category" is a story. It has weight at the beginning, a turn in the middle, and payoff at the end. It moves.

Every script the system produces must move. Not because movement is a stylistic preference, but because the human brain does not retain information that sits still. A viewer who watches a list of facts will forget them. A viewer who watches a problem get solved will remember the solution.

This applies to every format. Sizzle reels, explainers, internal platform demos, award submissions, brand films. The content changes. The requirement for movement does not.

## Topic vs. story

A topic is a subject. A story is a subject under pressure.

| Topic | Story |
|---|---|
| "We launched a new platform." | "Teams were losing three days per question. Then we built one place to find answers." |
| "Fuze Tea uses natural ingredients." | "People want energy but hate the crash. Fuze Tea found the ingredient list that solves both." |
| "Our sustainability program hit milestones." | "Five years ago we made a promise nobody believed. Here is what happened." |

The difference is always the same: the story version names a friction, applies pressure, and resolves it. The topic version just announces.

If you can state the script's content without naming a problem, a change, or an outcome, you have a topic, not a story. Go back.

## The non-negotiable three elements

Every script, regardless of format, runtime, or audience, must contain:

1. **A clear problem, tension, or unmet need.** Something is wrong, missing, incomplete, or at stake. The audience must feel why this matters before they hear what the solution is.

2. **A meaningful shift, solution, or intervention.** Something changes. A product launches. A strategy activates. A new capability arrives. The status quo breaks.

3. **An outcome, payoff, or clearer future state.** The world after the change is visibly different from the world before it. The audience can see what was gained.

If any of the three is missing, the script is broken.

- Without tension: the audience has no reason to care.
- Without a shift: the script is a description, not a narrative.
- Without an outcome: the audience does not know what to feel or do.

## How to find the tension

Every brief contains tension, even when it hides. Look for it in this order:

1. **Stated contradiction.** The brief says the audience wants X but experiences Y. "They want balance but feel overwhelmed." This is your opening.

2. **Implied gap.** The brief describes a new product or initiative. Ask: what existed before this? What was the audience doing without it? The gap between the old state and the new state is the tension.

3. **Competitive pressure.** The brief names competitors, market shifts, or category disruption. The tension is: the ground is moving, and we must move with it.

4. **Scale of ambition.** The brief describes a goal that sounds hard. The tension is the distance between where the brand is and where it wants to be.

5. **Human friction.** The brief describes a consumer behavior, habit, or need. Find the friction in that behavior. What is annoying, slow, confusing, or unsatisfying about the current experience?

If you truly cannot find tension in the brief, you have a weak brief. State the gap and propose a tension before writing.

## How to find the change

The change is whatever breaks the status quo established in Act 1. It answers: "And then what happened?"

- A product launched that did not exist before.
- A strategy shifted direction.
- A formula improved.
- A platform replaced three broken tools.
- A brand entered a new category.
- Results exceeded expectations.

The change must be specific. "We innovated" is not a change. "We reformulated Zero Sugar three times in four years until the taste scores matched Classic" is a change.

## How to find the outcome

The outcome is the new reality after the change. It answers: "So what?"

- Revenue grew by a specific number.
- Consumers shifted perception measurably.
- A team that took three days now takes three minutes.
- A brand that was dismissed is now the fastest-growing in its category.
- An audience that felt overwhelmed now feels in control.

The outcome should circle back to the tension. If Act 1 named the problem, Act 3 should show the problem resolved. The audience should feel the distance traveled.

## How to identify audience stakes

Ask: why should this specific audience care about this tension?

- If the audience is leadership: the stakes are strategic. Growth, market position, competitive advantage.
- If the audience is retail partners: the stakes are commercial. Sell-through, category growth, shopper behavior.
- If the audience is consumers: the stakes are personal. How their daily experience changes.
- If the audience is award judges: the stakes are proof. Evidence that the work delivered results.
- If the audience is internal teams: the stakes are operational. How their work gets better, faster, or clearer.

Name the stakes before writing. The stakes determine which details matter and which are filler.

## How to identify emotional movement

The audience should not feel the same way at the end as they did at the beginning. Map the emotional trajectory:

- **Opening emotion**: What does the tension make them feel? Frustrated? Curious? Skeptical? Overwhelmed?
- **Middle emotion**: What does the change make them feel? Surprised? Impressed? Hopeful? Convinced?
- **Closing emotion**: What does the outcome make them feel? Proud? Energized? Confident? Hungry to act?

If the emotional state is flat across all three acts, the script has no arc. Rewrite.

The emotional shift does not need to be dramatic. An explainer might move from "confused" to "clear." A case study might move from "skeptical" to "convinced." But the movement must exist.

## The default three-part arc

### Act 1: The problem that needs solving

Establish what is wrong, missing, or at stake. Create felt tension. Make the audience need the solution before you offer it.

- Name the friction, gap, or contradiction.
- Ground it in specific behavior, data, or human experience.
- Do not rush past this. If the audience does not feel the problem, they will not value the solution.
- Typical allocation: 20-25% of runtime.

### Act 2: The steps to the solution

Show the change happening. Walk through the key moves, features, pillars, or proof points that solve the problem named in Act 1.

- Each beat should build on the last, not just sit next to it.
- Progress should be visible. The audience should feel momentum.
- Data and proof deploy here, at the moments of highest need.
- This is where strategic frameworks recur as visual tracking devices.
- Typical allocation: 50-60% of runtime.

### Act 3: The outcome of the new change

Land the result. Show the world after the change. Resolve the tension from Act 1. Make the audience feel the distance traveled.

- Circle back to the opening tension. Show it resolved.
- Deploy the strongest metric or most human proof point.
- The visual motif reaches its final form.
- The closing line earns the end feeling.
- Typical allocation: 15-25% of runtime.


## Fast arc for :30 spots

- :00-:08 Problem / tension
- :08-:22 Change / proof / escalation
- :22-:30 Outcome / brand landing

If a :30 spot has four ideas, it has too many.

## Two useful shortcuts

### Product spot arc
Need -> sharper want -> product proof -> appetite payoff

### Explainer arc
Pain -> tool -> how it works -> better future state


## Arc behavior by script type

### Standard Sizzle

**Best opening behavior:** Lead with the boldest tension or the sharpest contradiction. Sizzles need to grab fast. Open with energy, stakes, or surprise. Name the "but" in the first 8 seconds.

**The middle's job:** Build momentum through escalating proof. Each pillar or beat should feel bigger, faster, or more impressive than the last. The sizzle middle is a crescendo, not a catalog.

**The ending's job:** Land with a gut-punch line that makes the audience feel the scale of what they just saw. The ending should make them want to rewatch or share.

**Most common failure mode:** The sizzle becomes a feature list. Every section gets equal weight. There is no build, no escalation, no climax. It feels like a deck read aloud.

### Explainer

**Best opening behavior:** Lead with the user's pain. What is broken, slow, or confusing about the current experience? Even a platform demo needs the audience to feel the problem before the walkthrough. Name the tool within the first 15 seconds, but only after the friction is felt.

**The middle's job:** Walk through the solution in the order the user would experience it. Each feature or capability should resolve a specific piece of the pain from Act 1. The middle is a guided tour, not a feature dump.

**The ending's job:** Show the transformed experience. What does the user's day look like now? How much time, effort, or confusion is gone? The ending should make the old way feel unacceptable.

**Most common failure mode:** The explainer skips Act 1 entirely and opens with "Meet [Platform Name]." No tension. No stakes. The audience watches a product tour they never asked for. The fix is always the same: start with the problem the platform solves.

### Case Study / Awards

**Best opening behavior:** Lead with the challenge or the goal that seemed hard to hit. Award judges want to see ambition and difficulty before they see results. Name what was at stake and why the outcome was not guaranteed.

**The middle's job:** Show the strategic and creative work that bridged the gap between challenge and result. This is where methodology, insight, and execution live. Each beat should feel like a deliberate choice, not an accident.

**The ending's job:** Deploy the results. Hard numbers. Measurable impact. Then zoom out to what the success means for the brand, category, or future strategy. The ending should make the judges feel the significance, not just the stats.

**Most common failure mode:** The case study buries the results at the very end after spending 80% of runtime on context. Or it leads with results and has nowhere to go. The fix: hint at the scale of results early ("what happened next surprised everyone"), deliver context and method in the middle, then pay off with full results at the close.

### Experiential / Consumer Journey

**Best opening behavior:** Lead with the human moment. What does the consumer feel, want, or lack before this experience begins? Ground it in a specific behavior, not an abstraction. Show the person, not the persona.

**The middle's job:** Walk the consumer through the experience. Each stage should shift their emotional state. The journey is not a map of touchpoints. It is a progression of feelings: curiosity to discovery to delight to connection.

**The ending's job:** Show the consumer changed. Not "inspired" in the generic sense. Show the specific new behavior, belief, or feeling they carry out. The product or experience should feel like the reason they are different.

**Most common failure mode:** The journey describes logistics instead of emotions. "First they enter the space. Then they see the display. Then they sample the product." That is a floor plan, not a story. The fix: at every stage, name what the consumer feels, not just what they see.

### Commercial / Brand Film

**Best opening behavior:** Lead with the human truth that makes the brand relevant. Not the brand's claim about itself. The real tension in people's lives that the brand resolves. This must feel observed, not manufactured.

**The middle's job:** Build the brand's case through accumulated moments, not arguments. Each beat should feel like evidence the audience discovers, not a point being made at them. The middle is seduction, not persuasion.

**The ending's job:** Arrive at the brand truth as if it were inevitable. The audience should feel like they reached the conclusion themselves. The tagline or brand line should land as confirmation of what they already feel, not as a reveal.

**Most common failure mode:** The brand film announces its thesis in the opening and then illustrates it for 60 seconds. There is no discovery, no build, no earning. The audience is told what to feel and then shown supporting footage. The fix: withhold the thesis. Let the moments build. Let the brand line land at the end as the answer to a question the audience has been asking since the opening.

## Arc Checks

Run these before finalizing any script:

### 1. Is there a real tension in the opening?

Can you name the problem, gap, friction, or contradiction in one sentence? If you cannot, the tension is missing or vague. "The market is evolving" is not tension. "Consumers want clean energy but do not trust the brands that sell it" is tension.

### 2. Does the middle progress or just describe?

Read only the Act 2 beats in order. Does each one build on the previous? Does the audience know more, feel more, or believe more with each beat? Or could you shuffle the beats without anyone noticing? If the order does not matter, you have a list, not a progression.

### 3. Does the ending resolve and land, or just stop?

Does the closing reference or resolve the tension from the opening? Does the audience feel the distance between where the story started and where it ended? Or does the script just run out of content and paste a tagline? An ending that could work on any video is not an ending.

### 4. Is there visible movement?

Compare the emotional state of the audience at the open, middle, and close. Are they different? If the audience feels the same way throughout, the script has no arc. Name the three emotional states. If you cannot, rewrite.

### 5. Could this opening or ending belong to any generic video?

Read the opening line in isolation. Could it open a competitor's video? A different brand's sizzle? A stock template? If yes, it is not specific enough. The same test applies to the closing line. Both must be rooted in this brief's specific tension and resolution.

### 6. Does the arc serve the assignment?

The arc must solve the brief, not just tell a pretty story. If the brief asks the audience to understand a new platform, the arc must move from confusion to clarity. If it asks them to feel proud of results, the arc must move from challenge to proof. Match the arc to the job.
