# Tone System

Tone is a required input, not a decoration. It is selected before writing begins and materially affects VO style, visual pacing, music direction, and edit rhythm.

Tone is not the same as Internal vs. External. Internal/External is a production routing decision (who sees it, what needs approval). Tone is how the piece feels regardless of audience.

An internal video can be Energetic. An external video can be Confident. Do not conflate the two.

## How tone works in the system

1. The user selects a tone from the dropdown before writing begins.
2. The tone sets parameters for writing, visuals, music, and pacing.
3. Every section of the script must hold the selected tone. Drift is a failure (see 12_gotchas).


## Common tone confusions

- **Product-Led vs Brand-Led**  
  Product-Led sells the thing.  
  Brand-Led sells the belief.

- **Commercial / Cinematic vs Emotion-Driven**  
  Commercial privileges image and restraint.  
  Emotion-Driven privileges human feeling and behavior.

- **Confident / Corporate vs Direct / Informational**  
  Confident argues.  
  Direct instructs.

## Tone stacking is not allowed

Do not write:
"Start cinematic, go energetic, end inspirational."

Pick one governing tone.
Variation inside the tone is fine. Switching tonal systems is not.


## Tone options

### Tech-Forward

**When to use:** Platform launches, product demos, innovation showcases, AI-assisted pieces, tool introductions.

- **VO density:** 70-80% of runtime. VO carries the explanation. Allow brief visual-only beats for UI demos and transitions.
- **VO style:** Clean, precise, efficient. Short declarative sentences. Terminology is specific but not jargon-heavy. Confidence without hype.
- **Visual pacing:** Medium-fast cuts, 2 to 4 seconds. UI animations, data visualizations, screen captures with motion. Clean compositions with graphic overlays.
- **Music:** 100 to 120 BPM. Ambient electronic, synth-driven, minimal percussion. Modern and polished. Sound design elements woven in.
- **Edit rhythm:** Crisp and logical. Each beat introduces, demonstrates, then moves on. Progress feels inevitable.
- **Emotional register:** Smart. Forward-looking. "This is how it works, and it works well."

### Emotion-Driven

**When to use:** Consumer stories, cause-related content, heritage pieces, human-centered campaigns, CSR films.

- **VO density:** 50-65% of runtime. Silence and music carry as much meaning as words. Let moments breathe without narration. VO re-enters at pivots and the landing.
- **VO style:** Personal, earned warmth. Room for vulnerability. Sentences breathe. First-person or close-third works. Earned emotion only... never manufactured.
- **Visual pacing:** Longer holds, 4 to 7 seconds. Handheld texture. Natural light. Breath between shots. Faces held long enough to read.
- **Music:** 70 to 95 BPM. Acoustic, piano-led, strings that swell late. Real instruments. Space between notes. The music earns its crescendo.
- **Edit rhythm:** Unhurried. Lets moments sit. Silence is a tool. The pace serves the feeling, not the clock.
- **Emotional register:** Specific and human. Not sentimental. "This is what it actually looks like."

### Product-Led

**When to use:** Product launches, line extensions, flavor/variant spotlights, retail-facing content, trade presentations.

- **VO density:** 70-80% of runtime. VO drives the benefit story. Visual-only beats reserved for hero product reveals and transitions.
- **VO style:** Direct and benefit-focused. Leads with what the product does, not what the brand believes. Specific claims backed by proof. Concise.
- **Visual pacing:** Medium cuts, 3 to 5 seconds. Hero product shots with motion. Macro details. Pack shots that transform. Clean transitions between features.
- **Music:** 95 to 115 BPM. Modern pop or electronic. Clean, polished, forward-moving. Not competing with the product for attention.
- **Edit rhythm:** Structured around product reveals. Each section builds to a visual payoff. Efficient, not rushed.
- **Emotional register:** Appetite. Desire. Clarity. "Here is what you get and why it matters."

### Brand-Led

**When to use:** Brand platform launches, positioning films, audience definition pieces, campaign anchors, manifesto-style content.

- **VO density:** 60-75% of runtime. VO builds the argument, but visual montage and music carry the cultural evidence. The brand reveal moment should land without VO competing.
- **VO style:** Elevated but not inflated. Declarative. Builds an argument through accumulation. Supers stack toward a platform reveal. The brand name earns its arrival.
- **Visual pacing:** Varies by section. Can mix fast cultural montage with slower brand moments. 2 to 5 seconds per shot. Builds in layers.
- **Music:** 90 to 120 BPM. Genre matches the brand's cultural space. Can be bold or restrained depending on the brand. Music has personality.
- **Edit rhythm:** Architectural. Each section is a brick in the argument. The pace accelerates toward the platform reveal, then holds for the landing.
- **Emotional register:** Conviction. Ownership. "This is who we are. This is why it matters."

### Commercial / Cinematic

**When to use:** Brand films, spec commercials, :30 and :60 spots, high-production pieces, AI-led cinematic work.

- **VO density:** 20-40% of runtime. Visuals and music are the primary storytelling tools. VO, when present, punctuates rather than narrates. Many cinematic pieces work best with zero VO.
- **VO style:** Minimal or absent. When present, poetic but restrained. Imagery over argument. Every word is deliberate. Can work with no VO at all, letting visuals and music carry the piece.
- **Visual pacing:** Deliberate. 3 to 8 second holds. Cinematic compositions. Controlled camera moves. Each frame is composed, not captured.
- **Music:** 70 to 100 BPM. Cinematic score, modern classical, or a licensed track that carries the piece. Music is a primary storytelling element.
- **Edit rhythm:** Film-like. Breathes. Transitions dissolve or morph rather than hard cut. The pace is about feeling, not information.
- **Emotional register:** Aesthetic conviction. "Watch this. Feel this."

### Human / Documentary

**When to use:** Employee stories, community spotlights, behind-the-scenes, testimonial-driven content, real-people narratives.

- **VO density:** 50-70% of runtime. Soundbites and interview clips replace traditional VO in many sections. Observational footage runs without narration. Let the person's words do the work.
- **VO style:** Conversational, unpolished on purpose. Can use interview soundbites as the VO spine. Casual phrasing. The voice sounds like a person, not a brand.
- **Visual pacing:** Handheld, observational. 4 to 8 seconds. Documentary texture. Available light. Imperfect framing is intentional.
- **Music:** 75 to 95 BPM. Acoustic, indie, lo-fi. Soft or absent. The music should feel like it wandered in, not like it was scored.
- **Edit rhythm:** Follows the person, not the structure. Pauses are real. Cuts happen when the moment is done, not on a beat grid.
- **Emotional register:** Authentic without saying "authentic." "Here is a real person. Listen."

### Confident / Corporate

**When to use:** Internal strategy films, leadership presentations, partner pitches, stakeholder updates, business reviews.

- **VO density:** 75-85% of runtime. VO is the primary delivery vehicle. Data and argument need narration. Brief pauses for data reveals and graphic transitions, but VO stays in the driver's seat.
- **VO style:** Measured, assured sentences. Slightly longer than most tones. Data-forward. Avoids hype. Professional without being stiff. Facts carry the weight.
- **Visual pacing:** Medium cuts, 3 to 5 seconds per shot. Clean compositions. Graphic frameworks. Data visualizations. Charts that animate with purpose.
- **Music:** 90 to 110 BPM. Ambient electronic, light piano, modern orchestral. Understated but forward-moving. Never competes with VO.
- **Edit rhythm:** Steady pulse. Each section lands cleanly before the next begins. No rushing. No drama unless the data warrants it.
- **Emotional register:** Competent. Clear-eyed. "We know what we are doing and here is the proof."

### Direct / Informational

**When to use:** Training videos, process explainers, onboarding content, how-to pieces, compliance and enablement.

- **VO density:** 80-90% of runtime. VO is the teaching tool. Nearly every second has narration. Pauses only for step demonstrations and screen recordings where the visual needs to be read without distraction.
- **VO style:** Clear, efficient, instructional. No filler. No metaphor. Sentences are short and functional. Steps are numbered or sequenced. The goal is comprehension, not impression.
- **Visual pacing:** Steady, 3 to 6 seconds. Screen recordings, diagrams, step-by-step visuals. Clean, uncluttered frames. Graphics support understanding.
- **Music:** 85 to 100 BPM. Light, unobtrusive. Corporate modern or ambient. Low in the mix. Can be absent entirely.
- **Edit rhythm:** Methodical. Each point lands, is demonstrated, then moves to the next. Pacing serves clarity.
- **Emotional register:** Helpful. Clear. "Here is what you need to know."

### Energetic / Upbeat

**When to use:** Event openers, sizzle reels, social content, celebration pieces, launch announcements, rally moments.

- **VO density:** 50-65% of runtime. Music and SFX are co-leads. VO punches in at key moments, then gets out of the way. Let the track and the visuals carry the energy between VO hits.
- **VO style:** Short, punchy sentences. Fragments land hard. Declarative. High confidence. No hedging. Exclamation is in the rhythm, not the punctuation.
- **Visual pacing:** Fast cuts, 1.5 to 3 seconds per shot. Layered frames. Split screens. Motion-heavy. Color-saturated.
- **Music:** 115+ BPM. Driving percussion. Electronic, hip-hop influenced, or pop. Drops and builds. The track is a primary element.
- **Edit rhythm:** Stacked, accelerating. Builds toward a peak, then one held beat at the end. Cut to music.
- **Emotional register:** Fired up. Competitive. Hungry. "We are here and we brought receipts."

### Inspirational

**When to use:** Award submissions, milestone celebrations, vision pieces, keynote openers, year-in-review films, legacy content.

- **VO density:** 60-75% of runtime. VO builds the argument, but the crescendo needs air. Visual-only beats expand in the final third as the emotional payoff lands. The landing line should arrive after a breath, not on top of continuous narration.
- **VO style:** Elevated but earned. Builds from grounded observation to aspirational landing. Contrast structures drive the arc: where we were, where we are, where we are going. The uplift is specific, not generic.
- **Visual pacing:** Starts measured, expands. 3 to 6 seconds early, longer holds at the close. The visual world opens up as the story grows.
- **Music:** 80 to 110 BPM. Orchestral build, modern classical, or anthemic indie. Starts sparse. The crescendo is saved for the final third.
- **Edit rhythm:** Patient early. Accelerates through proof. Breathes at the landing. The arc has shape.
- **Emotional register:** Pride. Conviction. Forward momentum. "Look at what we built. Now look at what comes next."

## Tone and the self-critique rubric

When running the self-critique (11), check: Does the script hold its tone from open to close? Common drift patterns:

- Energetic scripts that go soft in the middle
- Confident scripts that try to get emotional at the close without earning it
- Emotion-Driven scripts that slip into generic uplift language
- Commercial scripts that speed up because the writer got nervous about pacing
- Inspirational scripts that start inspirational instead of building to it

If the tone drifts, identify where and fix it. The tone should feel inevitable from first frame to last.

## Tone is not volume

Energetic does not mean loud. Commercial does not mean slow. Human does not mean soft. Each tone has its own version of intensity. An Emotion-Driven script can be devastating. An Energetic script can have a quiet beat that makes the next hit land harder.

Tone controls the texture of intensity, not the amount of it.
