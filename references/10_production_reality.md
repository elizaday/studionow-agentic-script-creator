# Production Reality

A strong script is aware of what production will actually require.

## Always consider:

- existing footage vs new shoot
- archival rights
- logos and likeness
- official approval needs
- controlled assets
- live event capture limits
- AI image/video consistency
- motion graphics load
- localization workload
- edit complexity
- timeline realism

## Flag likely approval or access issues

Examples:
- trophy or championship hardware
- executive likeness
- broadcast sports imagery
- partner marks
- celebrity appearances
- archival footage
- stadium or event footage
- city-specific branded activations
- historical recreations
- AI-generated public figure resemblance

## Asset realism rules

Do not casually write:
- private jet access
- stadium access
- packed public square
- controlled hero object shoot
- crowd-scale spectacle
- famous footage
unless the brief implies that access exists or the script clearly labels it as aspirational / subject to approval.

## AI-specific rules

When writing AI-assisted visuals:
- define visual logic clearly
- maintain one emotional and stylistic throughline
- avoid random prompt soup
- avoid tech-demo energy
- avoid hyper-real faces if continuity or ethics are a risk
- describe motion behavior, texture, and transitions
- state what must remain human-curated

## Stock-aware rules

If the concept depends heavily on stock:
- do not overspecify impossible coverage
- write toward available categories of footage
- avoid fake specificity that stock will not support
- build concept around what is actually attainable

## Runtime density assessment

Before committing to a runtime, count the major ideas, pillars, or sections the brief requires. Then apply:

- **1-2 major ideas**: 60 seconds works
- **3-4 major ideas**: 90 seconds minimum. Each idea needs setup + reveal + transition.
- **5+ major ideas**: 2 minutes, or cut ideas, or recommend companion pieces

When the brief gives a range (e.g., "60-120 seconds"), choose the runtime that lets every idea breathe. Do not default to the shortest option.

Each major idea needs three things to land:
1. **Setup**: Why does this matter? (3-5 seconds)
2. **Reveal**: The product, stat, or visual payoff (5-8 seconds)
3. **Transition**: How it flows into the next idea (2-3 seconds)

If you cannot fit all three for every section, you have too many sections for your runtime.

## Runtime honesty

If the brief asks for too much:
- simplify
- modularize
- prioritize
- or recommend companion assets

Do not solve by cramming. Cramming four pillars into 60 seconds produces a list, not a story.
