# Voice and Language

## Core voice

StudioNow voice is:
- clean
- direct
- sharp
- visual
- controlled
- unsentimental unless sentiment is earned
- active rather than passive

The writing should sound like someone with taste and production experience wrote it.

## Preferred sentence behavior

- declarative
- compressed
- contrast-driven
- rhythm-aware
- specific
- low on filler
- strong nouns and verbs

## Use contrast
Contrast creates movement.

Examples of contrast moves:
- We had momentum. Then the floor gave way.
- The shelf was full. None of it tasted right.
- It looked like a tour. It felt like arrival.
- This was not a history lesson. It was memory in motion.

## Use fragments carefully
Fragments are good when they land something:
- One team.
- No hard cuts.
- Hand to hand.
- Built to scale.

Do not overdo them.

## Use ellipses sparingly
Ellipses are for breath, not decoration.
Use only where a voiced pause genuinely helps.

## What to avoid completely

Avoid lines like:
- the most coveted prize
- coming to you
- your moment with greatness
- unforgettable journey
- world-class experience
- building anticipation
- bringing the future to life
- innovative solutions
- next-level
- game-changing
- immersive storytelling
- leverage
- best-in-class
- powerful brand moment
- create awareness and excitement
- where magic happens
- this is more than
- default avoid: not just X, but Y

These are weak because they are portable.
Portable language usually means low specificity.


## Speakability test

Read the line out loud.
If a smart producer would stumble, shorten it.
If it sounds like a deck headline with verbs added, rewrite it.

## No rhetorical fog

Avoid:
- empty questions
- thesisy throat-clearing
- category statements with no friction

Bad:
"What does flavor mean today?"

Better:
"The shelf was crowded. None of it felt worth grabbing."

## Bad vs better

Bad:
“The most coveted prize in football is coming to you.”

Better:
“The road to 2026 stops here first.”

Bad:
“We’re building anticipation for what’s to come.”

Better:
“This is where the tournament starts to feel real.”

Bad:
“A powerful celebration of connection.”

Better:
“Different tables. Same instinct.”

Bad:
“An unforgettable experience for fans.”

Better:
“A line forms before the doors open.”

## Rules for emotional writing

- Earn uplift. Do not announce it.
- Use human stakes instead of abstract values.
- Name the gesture, not the slogan.
- Prefer behavior over adjectives.
- Prefer image over claim.

### Emotional specificity

Do not write about feelings in the abstract. Write the specific human behavior that proves the feeling.

Bad: "Genuine human moments." (What moments? This is a placeholder.)
Good: "A father slides his daughter's plate closer, taps the Coke bottle twice before opening it. Their ritual."

Bad: "Authentic experiences with the product." (Generic.)
Good: "She holds the bottle against her neck before opening it. The condensation is the first relief."

Bad: "People connecting over meals." (Describes a category, not a moment.)
Good: "Four hands reach for the last wing. Three pull back. One doesn't."

Name the gesture. Name the behavior. Name the friction. Never describe an emotion when you can show the action that causes it. If your visual says "genuine" or "authentic," you have not been specific enough.

**Specificity means sharper, not longer.** One precise image replaces three vague ones. "A father taps the bottle twice" beats "families enjoying authentic shared moments together" and uses fewer words. If being more specific makes your script longer, you are adding detail instead of replacing vagueness. Cut the vague line entirely and put the sharp one in its place.

## The opening must find the tension

Every brief has a "but." A contradiction, a gap, a human friction that the product or initiative resolves. The opening must surface this tension in the first 8 seconds.

Structure: Human truth or desire > "But" or contradiction > Product/initiative as resolution

Bad: "People are looking for more." (statement, no stakes)
Good: "When they say 'yes' to life, they feel fulfilled. But too much 'yes' leaves them feeling overloaded." (felt contradiction)

Bad: "Innovation is changing the way we work." (generic, could open any video)
Good: "We had the data. We had the platform. But every decision still took six weeks." (specific, creates urgency)

If your opening could belong to a hundred other brand films, it has no tension. Find the "but."

**Critical: Override weak brief openings.** When the brief itself suggests an opening line or story flow, do NOT copy it verbatim. Test it against the tension rule first. If the brief says "Opening: People are looking for MORE," that is a prompt to find what "more" actually means, not permission to use a generic statement. Look deeper into the brief or attached materials for the real human contradiction. The deck almost always contains a better opening than the brief's summary suggests. Mine the attachments for the actual tension, then write a sharper version.


## Placeholder copy test

Delete any line that can survive unchanged after swapping:
- client name
- product name
- category

If the line still works, it is generic.

## Write transitions between sections

VO must connect sections, not just introduce them. Use contrast, callback, or momentum language to bridge ideas:

- **Contrast bridge**: "But taste was only the beginning." / "But great taste shouldn't require a sacrifice."
- **Callback bridge**: "And remember that fusion?" / "That same energy..."
- **Momentum bridge**: "Now multiply that across..." / "And we're not stopping there."

The audience should never feel a section "drop in cold." Every new idea must arrive as a consequence of what came before.

## Deploy data as ammunition

Mine every brief for specific numbers: market sizes ($88B, $102B), growth percentages, rankings, dollar figures. These are not decoration. They are proof deployed at pivot points.

Place data where emotional stakes are highest:
- At the introduction of a new pillar (makes the opportunity feel massive)
- At the proof of a claim (makes the result undeniable)
- At the turn from problem to solution (makes the gap feel urgent)

Bad: (no data used)
Bad: "The natural invigoration space is growing."
Good: "By entering the $88 Billion Natural Invigoration space, we are ready to deliver clean, plant-based energy."

## Rules for VO

VO should:
- carry the spine, not explain every frame
- leave room for images to do work
- sound speakable
- avoid stacked abstractions
- avoid fake-poetic mush
- avoid sounding like a deck headline read aloud
- connect sections with transition language, not just introduce topics
- surface tension before introducing solutions

If the visuals already say it, trim the VO.

## Rules for supers

Supers are a second storytelling channel, not captions.

Their job is to confirm, contrast, or extend. Not repeat what VO just said.

Super voice characteristics:
- ALL CAPS, 1–5 words, declarative, owned
- No hedging. No qualifiers. No "the" or "a" unless necessary.
- Each super should do exactly one thing: land a claim, name a player, punctuate a beat, or deliver a number

The colon structure for supers:
`[CONCEPT]: [PROOF]`
Examples from real StudioNow scripts:
- SPRITE SHOWERS: BIGGER THAN EVER.
- SPRITE PASS: HACKING THE SUBWAY.
- VOLUME: CRASHED by 50%

The colon creates a subject/payoff tension in a single line. Use it when the concept name and the proof are both worth holding separately.

Stacking supers as argument:
Supers can build one after another, each tightening the logic toward a reveal. This works for brand platform launches and audience definition scripts. Each super adds a layer: "OWN PATH → FREE → TRUE TO THEMSELVES → THE SELF-MANIFESTORS" ... until the platform name earns its arrival. Do not reveal the platform conclusion early. Let it land.

Do not overwrite supers. A super that needs eight words is a headline that couldn't commit.

## Data as emotional beat

Metrics belong inside the emotional beat, not in supporting copy.

A volume crash is not a statistic. It is the low point of the story. Treat it that way.

Bad:
"Sprite saw a significant volume decline in this period."

Better:
"VOLUME: CRASHED by 50%"

Bad:
"The team's efforts led to improved brand performance."

Better:
"AND BECAME #1 SPARKLING BRAND IN LATAM"

The metric IS the scene. Don't annotate it. Don't soften it. Don't explain it. Deliver it and move.

Rules:
- Name the specific number, ranking, or result as a declaration, not a data point
- Use scale words when they are true: crashed, zero, first, only, #1
- Put the proof where the emotion is highest, not in a footnote or stat card
- "AND" before a triumph result is a structural move. It signals arrival after contrast

## The "just" reframe

"We didn't just join culture. We created it."

The "just" reframe is a contrast move: acknowledge what was expected, then blow past it. The first clause grants the baseline. The second clause makes the real claim.

Use it when:
- the expected result is already impressive
- the actual result is a category jump above it
- you want to make the audience reset before landing the full scope

Do not use it for small differences. Only use it when the second clause genuinely can't be reached from the first.

## Earned platform reveals

Do not open with the platform name. Build to it.

The platform name (the tagline, the event name, the campaign line) lands hardest when it arrives after the argument is complete.

Structure: establish the human truth or the challenge → name the mechanism or the pivot → prove the outcome → then reveal the platform as the name for all of it.

"IT'S THAT FRESH" lands in the Sprite global script after the audience definition, the cultural claim, and the brand position have all been established. By the time it arrives, the audience knows exactly what it means. Earn the reveal.
